## Dollar Cost Averaging is Fine. Value Averaging is Better.

Most people know about Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA). You throw the same $326 into the market every single month regardless of what the market is doing. It is simple, boring, and works fine over long periods.

But there's a smarter cousin that almost nobody talks about: **Value Averaging.**

The concept comes from a Harvard professor named Michael Edleson. He published a book about it in the '90s, and it has quietly been outperforming DCA in academic studies ever since.

Here's the difference in one sentence: **DCA targets a fixed contribution. VA targets a fixed portfolio value.**

With DCA, you invest $326 every month no matter what. With VA, you set a value target for your portfolio at each checkpoint, and you invest whatever it takes to hit that target.

If the market dropped and your portfolio is below target? You invest more. You're buying cheap.

If the market ripped and your portfolio is above target? You invest less. Maybe almost nothing.

VA forces you to do what every financial advisor tells you to do but nobody actually does: **buy low, sell high.** The math does the disciplining for you.

## The Car Fund Portfolio

This isn't a growth-at-all-costs portfolio. This is a "I need this money in five years and I can't afford to lose it all" portfolio. Let me walk through each position.

**USAI (30%)** is the anchor. It's a bond aggregate ETF that spins off monthly income. Thirty percent of the fund sits here because I need something that won't fall off a cliff if the market has a tantrum. The distributions help smooth out the ride.

**QQQI (25%)** is a covered call ETF on the Nasdaq 100. You get exposure to big tech names while the fund sells call options on top and hands you the premium as income. It caps your upside a little, but the monthly distributions are thick. In a sideways or slightly up market, QQQI prints.

**IWMI (20%)** is the same covered call strategy but applied to small caps (Russell 2000). Small caps are beaten up right now, which means the options premiums are elevated. Higher vol = fatter premiums. I like owning this while small caps are cheap.

**GDX (10%)** is gold miners. With tariff chaos, central banks hoarding gold, and the dollar looking like it might need a nap, having 10% in gold miners gives the portfolio a hedge that actually moves when everything else is falling. Gold miners are operationally leveraged to the gold price, so a 10% move in gold can translate to 30-40% in miner earnings.

**BRK-B (10%)** is Berkshire Hathaway. Warren Buffett is sitting on $350 billion in cash right now. When the next crash comes, Berkshire will be the buyer of last resort. It's the ultimate fortress stock. It also pays zero dividends, so all returns compound tax-free inside the position.

**WEC (5%)** is WEC Energy Group. I live in Wisconsin. They literally keep my lights on. It's a regulated utility that pays a 3.5% dividend and has raised it for 20 straight years. Five percent of my car fund sitting in the company that sends me an electric bill feels poetic.

The blended portfolio gives me income from QQQI, IWMI, and USAI, growth from BRK-B and GDX, stability from USAI and WEC, and a gold hedge for chaos. Assuming 10% annualized returns across the whole thing is reasonable given the mix.

## VA vs DCA: Show Me the Numbers

Here's the side-by-side if both strategies assume 10% annual returns.

**DCA:** Invest $326 every month for 60 months. Total out of pocket: **$19,582.** The market grows it to $25,000.

**VA:** Start at $417/month, gradually decrease to $221/month as the portfolio grows. Total out of pocket: **$19,124.** Same $25,000 at the end.

VA saves you $458 over five years. That's not the point. The point is what happens when the market doesn't cooperate.

With DCA, if the market crashes in year 2, you're still putting in $326. You're buying cheap, but by accident.

With VA, if the market crashes in year 2, your portfolio is suddenly way below target. The formula tells you to invest $500, $600, maybe $700 that month. You're buying the dip on purpose, by design, with mathematical precision. And when the market recovers (and it always does), all those extra shares you bought at the bottom explode upward.

VA turns crashes into opportunities. DCA just says "same as last month." That's the difference.

This is the beautiful thing about VA. The grind gets easier over time. The first year is the hardest. Every year after that, the market picks up more of the load.

**Base Case (10% annual return):** This is the plan as designed. Monthly range from $417 down to $221. Total out of pocket: $19,124.

**Bull Case (15% annual return):** The market does the heavy lifting. Monthly range collapses from $417 to $82 by the final year. Total out of pocket drops to just $16,204.

Here's what I love about VA in all three scenarios: **you still hit $25,000.** The strategy auto-adjusts. In the bear case, you invest more at lower prices. In the bull case, you invest less and let the market work. Either way, the destination doesn't change. Only the cost of the ticket does.
