## Where I Disagree

1. **Adding sensory smell details.** No. Michael doesn't write sensory-rich literary fiction. He writes like a guy at a bar explaining something that just clicked. "The acrid scent of burning timber" is workshop creative writing. It doesn't fit his voice. His power comes from blunt observation, not atmosphere.

2. **"The hardest goddamn thing I ever learned – harder than..."** You used em dashes. Hard no. That's a non-negotiable constraint. And even restructured, adding "goddamn" to that specific sentence softens it. The original line is powerful BECAUSE it's controlled. It's the one place he doesn't swear. That restraint IS the emphasis. Swearing everywhere and then being measured at the climax hits harder.

3. **Splitting "they'll hand you a match and tell you it's a birthday candle" into two sentences.** The one-sentence version has a rhythm to it. It's a punchline. Breaking it adds a pause that deflates the impact. The staccato works elsewhere but not on your best joke.

4. **"Or so I told myself" after the matches line.** This undercuts the honesty. Michael isn't second-guessing the telling. He's telling you what he believed at the time and then correcting it with the next line ("But a lodgepole pine isn't lighting matches because it hates the forest..."). That's already the self-reflection. Adding another layer makes it self-conscious.

5. **"It's a fool's errand" after the 2am Googling section.** Michael doesn't label things with idioms. He shows you why it's foolish with the next paragraph. Trust the reader.
