Most traders are obsessed with finding the right stock.

But the better question is:

Is this stock in the right neighborhood?

William O’Neil taught that the majority of leading stocks are usually found in leading industries.

That matters because studies show that 37% of a stock’s price movement is tied to the performance of its industry group, and another 12% comes from the strength of its broader sector.

In other words, roughly half of a stock’s move can be influenced by the strength of the group it belongs to.

Then add the “M” in CAN SLIM: market direction.

O’Neil also taught that roughly three out of four stocks follow the overall market trend.

So if you are buying a stock in a weak market, inside a weak sector, inside a weak industry group, you are not just making a stock pick.

You are fighting probability.

That is why I put so much emphasis on:





Market direction.



Sector strength.



Industry group leadership.



Relative strength.

Leading stocks inside leading groups.

Recently, that has meant paying close attention to areas like technology and semiconductors, because that is where a lot of the real leadership has been.

The goal is not just to find a good company.

The goal is to find a strong stock, in a strong group, aligned with the strongest market theme, while the general market is supporting risk.

Before buying a stock, ask:





Is the market helping or hurting me?



Is the sector leading or lagging?



Is the industry group attracting money?



Is this stock one of the strongest names in that group?

Because the best stock ideas usually do not happen in isolation.

Leadership clusters.

Money rotates.

The strongest stocks are often found where the strongest institutional demand is already flowing.

One of the biggest misconceptions in trading is that you need to be right all the time.

You do not.

What you need is the ability to keep losses small when you are wrong and press your advantage when you are right.

That is one of the biggest lessons Stanley Druckenmiller described learning from George Soros in Jack Schwager’s The New Market Wizards.

Druckenmiller said Soros taught him that when conviction is high, “you have to go for the jugular.”

He also called Soros “the best loss taker I’ve ever seen.”

That combination is powerful:

Be aggressive when you are right.

Walk away quickly when you are wrong.

I have made plenty of wrong calls on individual stocks. I am wrong often.

But the goal is not to be right on every name.

The goal is to structure your trading so the small losses stay small, and the winners have enough room to pay for the losers and then some.

I mentioned CIEN earlier because it had the kind of relative strength, leadership, and constructive price action I look for.

Since then, it has continued to show why holding real leaders matters.

One real leader can make up for a lot of small losses.

But only if you do two things:





Cut the trades that are not working.



Hold the trades that are.

That is the hard part.

Most traders do the opposite.

They give losing trades too much room and cut winning trades too quickly.

The best traders understand the math is not about being right all the time.

It is about making sure that when you are right, it actually matters.

### @darktrader999 (Dr. Scott Brown, Ph.D.)
> Kahneman and Tversky changed everything in 1979.

Their prospect theory paper is the most cited work in the history of economics outside of pure theory. It documented something that every serious trader already knew from experience but that mainstream economics had refused to acknowledge formally. People do not make decisions under uncertainty the way expected utility theory predicts. They overweight small probabilities. They feel losses roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains. They evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms. They are risk-seeking in the domain of losses and risk-averse in the domain of gains in ways that produce systematic and predictable departures from rational choice.

The four-fold pattern prospect theory predicts maps almost perfectly onto retail options behavior. Low probability of a large gain — the out of the money call expiring in seven days — attracts risk-seeking behavior even when the expected value is deeply negative. That is the lottery ticket. Boyer and Vorkink measured the cost of that behavior precisely. Ten to fifty percent per week. Consistently. Across thirteen years of data and 1.6 million option observations.

Prospect theory explains why the lottery ticket gets bought. The Boyer-Vorkink paper measures what it costs.

Together they form the most complete account available of why most retail options traders lose by design rather than by bad luck.

But here is what almost nobody in the behavioral finance literature discusses honestly.

The edifice has been under serious assault for forty years and the assault has been more successful than the behavioral economics establishment typically acknowledges.

Machina's Challenge

Mark Machina was not a critic of prospect theory in the conventional sense. He did not argue that Kahneman and Tversky were wrong about human behavior. He argued something more subtle and ultimately more damaging to the policy implications of their work.

In his landmark 1982 paper Machina showed that the behavioral patterns prospect theory describes — including the Allais paradox, the certainty effect, the reflection effect — do not actually require abandoning the rational choice framework entirely. They can be accommodated within a generalized utility theory that assumes smooth preferences rather than the strict independence axiom of expected utility theory.

This matters enormously for what you can conclude from the behavioral observations.

If Machina is right — and the mathematical demonstration is rigorous enough that it has never been successfully refuted on technical grounds — then the departures from expected utility that Kahneman and Tversky documented are not evidence of fundamental irrationality. They are evidence of a richer preference structure than the simplest version of expected utility theory assumed. Preferences that are locally rational, globally coherent, and amenable to systematic modeling — just not the specific model that dominated economics in 1979.

The implication is significant. If behavior under uncertainty can be modeled as generalized rational choice rather than as irrational deviation from a normative standard, then the entire apparatus of behavioral economics as a corrective to human irrationality needs to be reconsidered.

Machina also identified a specific technical problem in prospect theory that has never been fully resolved. The probability weighting function — the mechanism by which people overweight small probabilities — causes time inconsistency. An investor's optimal strategy at one point in time may be systematically rejected as suboptimal at a later point in time even without any new information arriving. This is not a minor technical issue. It means prospect theory cannot generate stable predictions about dynamic choice — which is precisely what financial markets require.

The More Recent Falsification Attempts

The empirical attack on prospect theory has intensified in the past two decades and the results are considerably more mixed than the behavioral economics literature typically presents.

The replication crisis in psychology hit behavioral economics hard. Several of the foundational demonstrations of loss aversion — perhaps the most influential single finding from prospect theory — have failed to replicate reliably across different populations, different elicitation methods, and different cultural contexts.

David Gal and Derek Rucker published a direct challenge to loss aversion in 2018 arguing that the original experimental designs confounded loss aversion with a much simpler phenomenon — status quo bias, the general preference for inaction over action regardless of whether losses are involved. When the experimental designs are modified to separate these two effects the specific signature of loss aversion becomes considerably weaker and in some studies disappears entirely.

Eldad Yechiam and colleagues have produced a series of papers showing that loss aversion in attention and arousal — people pay more attention to losses than gains — does not translate reliably into loss aversion in choice behavior. The psychological reality of loss salience is robust. The behavioral prediction that people will systematically sacrifice expected value to avoid losses is not.

Perhaps most damaging is the growing body of evidence that loss aversion coefficients — the ratio measuring how much more intensely losses are felt relative to equivalent gains — vary enormously across individuals, contexts, stakes levels, and elicitation methods. Kahneman and Tversky reported a coefficient of approximately two — losses feel roughly twice as bad as equivalent gains feel good. More recent meta-analyses find coefficients ranging from below one to above four across different studies. A parameter that varies that widely across conditions is not measuring a stable psychological property. It is measuring something that depends heavily on how you ask the question.

The Deeper Problem

The falsification literature points toward something more fundamental than a collection of failed replications.

Prospect theory was built on laboratory experiments using hypothetical choices with small stakes. Subjects were asked what they would choose between Option A and Option B — gambles involving amounts of money small enough that the choices were essentially consequence-free. The behavioral patterns Kahneman and Tversky documented are real and robust in that specific context.

The problem is that financial markets are not that context.

In financial markets choices have real consequences. Participants have strong incentives to learn from their mistakes. Markets provide immediate and unambiguous feedback on whether a decision was correct. Experienced participants develop heuristics and rules — exactly the kind of systematic methodology the Turtle experiment demonstrated can be taught — that override the naive psychological responses prospect theory predicts.

This is the point that Machina's framework anticipates. Rational choice models with smooth preferences predict that agents who face repeated consequential decisions with clear feedback will learn to behave in ways that approximate rational choice even if their naive psychological responses do not. The market selects for the traders who overcome the lottery ticket preference. The ones who cannot afford to keep buying lottery tickets at negative expected value eventually run out of capital.

The Boyer-Vorkink finding supports both sides of this tension simultaneously and it is worth reading carefully.

The lottery ticket buyers lose consistently. That is the prospect theory prediction confirmed empirically. But the losses do not arbitrage away. The lottery ticket buyers keep buying despite the negative expected returns. That persistence is what prospect theory predicts — the probability weighting that makes the lottery ticket feel attractive does not respond to feedback the way a rational learning model would predict.

So the market simultaneously selects against lottery ticket buying — the buyers lose — and fails to eliminate it — new buyers continuously replace the ones who have been wiped out. The behavioral pattern is robust at the population level even as it is punished at the individual level.

That is a more complicated picture than either pure rationality or pure irrationality predicts. It is the picture Machina's framework actually anticipates — locally irrational responses that are globally stable because they are continuously replenished by new market entrants who have not yet paid the tuition.

### @darktrader999 (Dr. Scott Brown, Ph.D.)
> Thorstein Veblen may be the strangest mind American economics ever produced.

He saw the entire architecture of modern consumer society in 1899 — before the automobile, before television, before social media, before any of the mechanisms that would accelerate and amplify everything he described had been invented. He understood that people do not consume to satisfy needs. They consume to communicate position. That the real economy is not an exchange of goods and services but a continuous performance of status conducted through objects, leisure, and display.

He called it conspicuous consumption and the phrase entered the language so completely that most people who use it today have never read the book it came from.

The Theory of the Leisure Class should have made him the most celebrated economist of his generation. Instead it made him unemployable.

The problem was not the ideas. The problem was that Veblen applied them without exception.

He did not write about the leisure class as an abstraction. He wrote about the people sitting across the table from him at faculty dinners. The university administrators who justified their salaries through ceremonial display rather than productive output. The donors who endowed buildings with their names rather than funded research that might produce uncomfortable results. The academic economists who performed the rituals of scholarly respectability while carefully avoiding any conclusion that might disturb the class interests of the people funding their institutions.

He saw through all of it and he said so.

In print.

Repeatedly.

The academic establishment responded the way establishments always respond to accurate description. They made his career as difficult as possible while being unable to actually refute his arguments. He was passed from institution to institution — Cornell, Chicago, Stanford, Missouri — usually asked to leave or quietly encouraged not to return. His personal life was unconventional enough to give administrators the pretext they needed when the intellectual case against him was too difficult to make directly.

He died in 1929 a few months before the crash that vindicated virtually everything he had written about the fragility of an economy built on status performance rather than productive value. He died poor, isolated, largely unread by the public he had tried to describe.

Here is what makes Veblen genuinely brilliant rather than merely clever.

He understood something that most social critics miss entirely. The behavior he described was not irrational. It was perfectly rational within the actual incentive structure of human social life. People signal status through consumption because status is real, social recognition is real, and the returns to being perceived as high status are real and measurable. The Maserati in the garage is not vanity. It is information transmission. It communicates a position in the social hierarchy in under three seconds to a complete stranger in a way that a CV, a publication record, or a brokerage statement cannot.

Veblen was not criticizing irrationality. He was describing a second economy running parallel to the official one — an economy of recognition and position conducted through objects — that the mainstream economics of his time refused to acknowledge existed.

That refusal was itself a Veblenian act. The economists who dismissed him were protecting their own status by ignoring an analysis that would have revealed the status-seeking embedded in their own professional behavior.

He was excluded for the same reason whistleblowers are always excluded. Not because he was wrong. Because he was right about the specific people in the room.

The cruelest irony is this. Veblen's ideas are now taught in every serious sociology and economics department in the world. His vocabulary — conspicuous consumption, the leisure class, invidious distinction, pecuniary emulation — is the foundational language of how we discuss status and wealth. His framework was extended by Bourdieu, applied by behavioral economists, verified by empirical research across a dozen disciplines.

He built the room that everyone else now sits in.

He just was not allowed to sit in it himself.

> Pierre Bourdieu spent most of his career trying to explain why the social world reproduces itself so reliably.

Why do the children of doctors become doctors? Why do the children of professors become professors? Why does accumulated advantage compound across generations in ways that look like merit from the inside and look like inheritance from the outside. His answer was capital — not just the economic kind but three kinds operating simultaneously and converting into each other under the right conditions?

Economic capital is money and assets. The portfolio. The real estate. The brokerage statements.

Cultural capital is knowledge, credentials, and what Bourdieu called habitus — the internalized dispositions that allow you to navigate institutions, recognize what is valued, and perform competence in ways that get recognized as legitimate. The PhD. The publication record. The forty-four years of systematic study that produces pattern recognition so deep it no longer feels like analysis.

Social capital is networks, relationships, and the accumulated trust of people whose recognition matters. The Rotary history. The academic collaborations. The twenty-one year friendship with an attorney who restacks your notes on a Sunday afternoon.

Bourdieu's central insight was that these three forms of capital convert into each other — but not automatically and not without a foundation. Economic capital alone does not produce social recognition. Cultural capital alone does not produce financial independence. The conversion requires the right conditions and the right sequence.

This is where options trading becomes the most unlikely Bourdieusian case study imaginable.

What Options Trading Actually Is in Bourdieu's Framework

Most retail options traders enter the market with economic capital — a funded account — and no cultural capital specific to the domain. They do not know how to read institutional footprints in the volume record. They cannot distinguish between a genuine breakout and random price noise. They have not internalized the habitus of systematic methodology through years of documented practice. They are economically capitalized and culturally impoverished within the specific field of options trading.

Bourdieu would predict exactly what the Boyer-Vorkink paper documented empirically. Those traders lose. Not because they are unintelligent in general but because they lack the domain-specific cultural capital that would allow them to navigate the field competently. They buy lottery tickets — high skewness options with terrible expected returns — because without the cultural capital to read the tape they cannot identify the specific positioning that actually generates asymmetric returns. The lottery ticket feels like the right move because it looks like the high upside bet. The cultural capital required to understand why it is the wrong move is precisely what they do not have.

The Conversion Sequence That Actually Works

What twenty-five years of systematic options trading produced in my own case is a very specific capital conversion sequence that Bourdieu's framework describes precisely.

It began with cultural capital accumulated through academic finance — the PhD, the teaching, the research, the ability to read a Journal of Finance paper and understand not just what it says but what it means for how to position a portfolio. That cultural capital was the foundation.

It converted into trading methodology — the Darvas framework, the Wyckoff volume analysis, the three signal entry, the mechanical thirty day exit. Methodology is cultural capital in its most practical form. It is internalized knowledge that produces different behavior in the same market environment from someone without it.

The methodology applied consistently across twenty-five years — through the burnout era, through the 2022 drawdown to $86,176, through the recovery to $3.48 million — converted cultural capital into economic capital. The brokerage statements are the record of that conversion. $9 million in documented matched gains is what cultural capital looks like when it has been systematically applied to a domain where most participants lack it.

The economic capital then converted into symbolic capital. The Maserati. The townhouse five miles from the Magic Kingdom. The trading lounge with the holographic epoxy floor and the Pixoticker running sixteen symbols on the wall. These are not consumption for its own sake. They are the visible form of a conversion that ran from knowledge through methodology through documented performance through material result.

The Part Bourdieu Got Complicated

Here is where Bourdieu's framework introduces a tension that is worth sitting with honestly.

Bourdieu argued that newly acquired economic capital does not automatically produce social recognition — especially not among established elites who distinguish between old capital and new capital, between inherited cultural habitus and acquired credentials, between people who grew up in the room and people who earned their way into it.

The nouveau riche problem. Conspicuous consumption that marks you as new money rather than elevating you into the existing hierarchy.

What makes the options trading case unusual is that the cultural capital was not acquired after the economic capital. It preceded it by decades. The PhD, the publication record, the academic methodology, the pattern recognition developed over forty-four years of Kyokushin karate training the same neural architecture that reads market structure — all of that was in place before the Maserati.

When the economic capital arrived it did not land on bare ground. It landed on a foundation of cultural capital that was already recognized as legitimate by the institutions that matter — universities, journals, scholarly networks, professional organizations.

That is the specific Bourdieusian condition under which economic capital converts cleanly into symbolic capital and social recognition rather than producing the nouveau riche effect he described. The combination is what works. Not money alone. Not credentials alone. The sequence and the foundation.

Bourdieu's framework suggests something uncomfortable about retail options trading that the Boyer-Vorkink paper confirms empirically.

The market is a field in Bourdieu's sense. It has its own rules, its own valued forms of capital, its own mechanisms for distributing rewards to those who possess the right kind of cultural capital and extracting it from those who do not.

That habitus cannot be downloaded. It cannot be purchased. ### @darktrader999 (Dr. Scott Brown, Ph.D.)
> Richard Dennis proved something in 1983 that the entire financial industry has spent forty years trying to ignore.

Trading skill is not talent. It is not intuition. It is not the product of some innate gift for reading markets that some people are born with and others are not. It is a set of rules that can be written down, handed to a stranger, and produce millions of dollars in verified profits within months.

He proved it with a bet.

Dennis had made over $200 million trading commodity futures by his early thirties. His partner William Eckhardt believed the ability to trade was innate — you either had it or you did not. Dennis believed it could be taught to anyone. They decided to settle the argument empirically rather than philosophically. They would recruit complete strangers, teach them a systematic methodology in two weeks, give them real money to trade, and see what happened.

They called the recruits Turtles. The name came from a turtle farm Dennis had visited in Singapore. He said he would grow traders the way the farm grew turtles — in quantity, from scratch, through a controlled process.

What happened next is the part the financial industry does not want to discuss.

The Turtles made $175 million in the following four years.

Not some of them. Most of them. Strangers with no trading background, no finance credentials, no special access to information, no proprietary technology. Two weeks of instruction. Real capital. Documented results.

What Dennis Actually Taught Them

This is where the story gets specific and the specificity is what matters.

The Turtle rules were not complex. They were not the product of sophisticated quantitative modeling or advanced mathematics. They were a systematic methodology built around a small number of precise principles applied without exception.

Trade in the direction of the trend. Enter on breakouts to new highs or lows. Size positions according to the volatility of the underlying instrument — larger positions in less volatile markets, smaller positions in more volatile ones. Exit when the trend reverses by a defined amount. Never override the system.

That was essentially the complete methodology. Entry rules. Position sizing rules. Exit rules. No discretion. No intuition. No feel for the market. Pure mechanical execution of a written system applied consistently regardless of what the trader personally believed was about to happen.

The position sizing rule deserves particular attention because it was the most counterintuitive element and the one most traders — even serious ones — consistently get wrong.

Dennis used a measure called N — essentially the average true range of the instrument over the past twenty days — to determine how many contracts to trade. The rule was to size each position so that one N of movement represented exactly one percent of total account equity. This meant positions were automatically smaller in volatile markets and larger in stable ones. It meant the system self-adjusted to risk rather than requiring the trader to make a judgment call about how much risk they were taking.

The Turtles did not decide how big to trade. The system decided. Their job was to execute.

The Thing Nobody Talks About

Here is the part of the Turtle story that is genuinely important and almost never discussed seriously.

Several of the Turtles eventually stopped following the rules.

Not immediately. Not obviously. But gradually, as they accumulated real money and real losses and real psychological pressure, some of them began to deviate. They would skip entries that felt wrong. They would hold positions longer than the exit rules specified because they believed the trend would resume. They would size positions differently from what the formula dictated because they had a feeling about this particular trade.

Their results deteriorated in direct proportion to their deviation from the rules.

The ones who continued to follow the system precisely — who treated themselves as execution mechanisms rather than decision makers — continued to perform. The ones who began to trust their judgment over the methodology began to lose.

Dennis watched this happen and drew the conclusion that the research on human decision-making under uncertainty had been pointing toward for decades. The enemy of systematic trading is not the market. It is the trader's own psychology operating on the system from the inside.

The rules were not the difficult part. Following them when every instinct said not to — that was the difficult part. That is always the difficult part.

Why This Is Uncomfortable for the Financial Industry

The Turtle experiment created a problem that Wall Street has never fully resolved.

If a complete stranger can be taught to generate millions of dollars in trading profits in two weeks using a system written on a piece of paper, what exactly are active fund managers selling?

Not skill in the sense of innate talent. Not proprietary insight. Not superior judgment. The Turtle experiment demonstrated that systematic rule-following by a trained novice outperforms discretionary judgment by experienced professionals across a broad range of market conditions.

The active management industry charges fees — often substantial ones — on the premise that professional judgment adds value above what a mechanical system would produce. The Turtle experiment is a direct empirical challenge to that premise. It suggests that the judgment is not the valuable thing. The discipline to follow the system when judgment screams to deviate — that is the valuable thing.

That conclusion is worth approximately $20 trillion in annual active management fees globally. Which may explain why it receives less attention than it deserves.

What Dennis Understood That Almost Nobody Else Did

Richard Dennis understood something about markets that took behavioral economics decades to document formally.

Markets are not primarily moved by information. They are moved by the aggregate behavior of participants responding to information — and that behavior is systematically predictable in ways that a disciplined rule-following system can exploit.

Trends exist because human psychology responds to price movement in consistent and exploitable ways. People chase strength. They panic at weakness. They hold losers too long and sell winners too early. They override their systems when the psychological pressure becomes uncomfortable. They confuse activity with edge.

A mechanical system that does the opposite — that buys strength when it is confirmed, cuts losses immediately when the trend fails, holds winners as long as the trend continues, and never deviates from the rules regardless of how uncomfortable the position feels — exploits the aggregate psychological failures of everyone else in the market.

Dennis did not discover this. Livermore understood it. Darvas understood it. Wyckoff understood it. What Dennis did that nobody before him had done was prove it was teachable — that the understanding could be transferred from one mind to another through explicit instruction rather than requiring decades of painful personal discovery.

He handed twenty-three strangers a rulebook and turned them into millionaires.

The rulebook still works.

The discipline to follow it is still the hard part.

The market is still full of people who cannot.

Es verdad que el S&P 500 está caro si lo miramos como un todo, pero esa media oculta una dispersión brutal entre sectores. Ya no estamos en ese momento donde todo sube porque sí; ahora lo que manda es la calidad del negocio y saber distinguir dónde el precio está justificado y dónde el pesimismo nos está regalando una oportunidad.

Si nos fijamos en los sectores que parecen más "caros", como la inteligencia artificial o el aeroespacial y defensa, vemos que esas valoraciones exigentes no son solo humo. En la IA tenemos balances sólidos y un salto de productividad que ya se nota en los beneficios, y en defensa, las carteras de pedidos están llenas para los próximos años por culpa de la inestabilidad geopolítica que nos rodea. Son sectores donde pagas más, pero lo haces porque hay flujos de caja visibles y una demanda que no va a desaparecer mañana. No es comprar por comprar, es entender que ahí el crecimiento tiene unos cimientos muy reales.

Sin embargo, en la otra cara de la moneda tenemos sectores como el de la salud, donde las expectativas están mucho más moderadas y los múltiplos son más razonables. A pesar de que la innovación en fármacos y dispositivos médicos no para de crecer y que la demanda a largo plazo es indiscutible, el ruido político y las dudas a corto plazo han castigado al sector. Aquí es donde el enfoque "bottom-up", o ir empresa a empresa, cobra todo el sentido del mundo. Mientras el mercado general parece caro, hay rincones donde los fundamentales siguen intactos y el castigo ha sido excesivo.
