## Dollar Cost Averaging is Fine. Value Averaging is Better.

Most people know about Dollar Cost Averaging. You throw the same $326 into the market every single month regardless of what the market is doing. Simple, boring, works fine over long periods.

DCA is like driving with a brick on the gas pedal. Same speed, same amount, no matter what the road looks like.

But there's a smarter cousin almost nobody talks about: **Value Averaging.**

The concept comes from a Harvard professor named Michael Edleson. Published a book about it in the '90s. It has quietly been outperforming DCA in academic studies ever since. And yes, I'm about to explain cruise control to you. I know. Bear with me.

**VA is adaptive cruise control.** You set your destination ($25,000). You set your timeline (five years). The system adjusts the throttle every month based on conditions. Market dropped and your portfolio is below target? Floor it, buy more. Market ripping and your portfolio is ahead of schedule? Ease off, let it ride.

Here's the difference in one sentence: **DCA targets a fixed contribution. VA targets a fixed portfolio value.**

It forces you to do what every financial advisor tells you to do but nobody actually does: buy low and sell high. The math does the disciplining for you. It's the financial version of "one day at a time." You don't try to predict the market's next binge or its euphoric rally. You just show up, look at the number, and do the next right thing.

## The Setup

**Goal:** $25,000
**Timeline:** 60 months (5 years)
**Assumed Return:** 10% annually (0.797% monthly)
**Strategy:** Value Averaging with a linear value path

The value path is simple. I want $25,000 at month 60. So each month, my target portfolio value increases by $416.67.

Month 1: portfolio should be worth $417
Month 12: portfolio should be worth $5,000
Month 30: portfolio should be worth $12,500
Month 60: portfolio should be worth $25,000

Each month, I check what the portfolio is actually worth after market returns, and deposit whatever it takes to hit that target. Market cooperated? Deposit less. It didn't? Deposit more. The destination never changes. Only the cost of gas does.

## VA vs DCA: Show Me the Numbers

![VA vs DCA Comparison](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/mphinance/mphinance/main/docs/substack/images/va_vs_dca_comparison.png)

Here's the side-by-side assuming 10% annual returns for both strategies.

**DCA:** Invest $326 every month for 60 months. Total out of pocket: **$19,582.** Market grows it to $25,000.

**VA:** Start at $417/month, gradually decrease to $221/month as the portfolio grows. Total out of pocket: **$19,124.** Same $25,000 at the end.

VA saves you $458 over five years. That's not the point though.

The point is what happens when the market doesn't cooperate.

With DCA, if the market crashes in year 2, you're still putting in $326. You're buying cheap, but by accident, like stumbling into a good decision.

With VA, if the market crashes in year 2, your portfolio is suddenly way below target. The formula tells you to invest $500, $600, maybe $700 that month. You're buying the dip on purpose, by design, with mathematical precision. And when the market recovers (and it always does), all those extra shares you bought at the bottom explode upward.

It forces you to do the hard thing when every instinct says run. Anybody in recovery knows that feeling.

VA turns crashes into opportunities. DCA just says "same as last month." That's the entire difference.

## The Month-by-Month Mechanics

Let me show you exactly how this works so it's not abstract.

**Month 1:** Portfolio is worth $0. Target: $417. You invest $417.

**Month 2:** Portfolio grew to $420 (0.8% monthly return). Target: $833. You invest $413. A little less than month 1 because the market helped.

**Month 6:** Portfolio grew to $2,073. Target: $2,500. You invest $427. Wait, that's more than month 1? Yes. If the market underperformed, VA tells you to invest more. That's the system working exactly as designed.

**Month 12:** Portfolio grew to $4,596. Target: $5,000. You invest $404. The portfolio is bigger now, generating its own return, so contributions start to shrink.

**Month 36 (Year 3):** Portfolio grew to $14,709. Target: $15,000. You invest $291. At this point the portfolio is generating about $117/month in returns on its own.

**Month 60 (Year 5):** Portfolio grew to $24,793. Target: $25,000. You invest $207. The portfolio is generating $198/month in returns. Your contribution is barely a rounding error. The market is doing almost all the work.

This is the compounding curve everyone talks about but nobody shows you in actual dollar terms.

## What If the Market Doesn't Hit 10%?

![Scenario Analysis](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/mphinance/mphinance/main/docs/substack/images/va_scenario_analysis.png)

I asked Sam to stress-test this.

**Bear Case (5% annual return):** Monthly contributions stay tighter, between $388 and $360. Total out of pocket climbs to $22,389. You're investing more, but buying at lower prices the entire time. When the market eventually normalizes, those cheap shares pay off hard.

**Base Case (10% annual return):** The plan as designed. Monthly range from $417 down to $221. Total out of pocket: $19,124.

**Bull Case (15% annual return):** Monthly range collapses from $417 to $82 by the final year. Total out of pocket drops to $16,204. The market does the heavy lifting and you barely have to contribute.

Here's what I love about VA across all three scenarios: **you still hit $25,000.** The strategy auto-adjusts. Bad road conditions? You invest more and buy cheaper. Smooth highway? Ease off and let momentum carry you. Either way, the destination doesn't change. Only the cost of gas does.
