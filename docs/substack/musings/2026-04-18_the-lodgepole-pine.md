# The Lodgepole Pine

*How to love a lodgepole pine, without getting burned.*

*Michael's Musings, April 18, 2026*

---

There's a tree in Yellowstone called the lodgepole pine. I think about it a lot.

If you've driven through Wyoming or Montana or certain parts of the Rockies, you've seen them. Thin, tall, packed so tight together they look like God planted a fence. They grow in massive stands, millions of them, crowding each other out, fighting for the same little scraps of light. From a distance they're beautiful. Up close they're kind of desperate.

But here's the thing about lodgepole pines that I can't get out of my head.

They need fire to survive.

Not "tolerate fire." Not "bounce back from fire." They literally *need* it. Their cones are sealed shut with resin so thick that no amount of wind, rain, or sunshine will open them. The seeds inside are perfectly viable. Ready to grow. Full of potential. But they're locked up. Glued shut by the tree's own biology.

The only thing that opens them is extreme heat. We're talking forest fire heat. The kind of heat that torches everything else around them.

When a fire rips through, the resin melts, the cones crack open, and a million seeds hit the scorched earth. The lodgepole is the first tree to colonize a burned landscape. While everything else is ash, the lodgepole is already sprouting. It evolved to be first on the scene after total destruction.

Scientists call the cones "serotinous." I had to look that up. It means "late to open." Basically, the tree holds its future hostage until disaster shows up.

Think about that. The tree's entire reproductive strategy depends on catastrophe. Without fire, no seeds. Without destruction, no new growth. The lodgepole pine is biologically wired to need the worst thing that could happen to it.

---

Okay. I'm going to stop pretending I'm David Attenborough for a second.

You see what I'm doing here, right? Grown man writing a nature fable like he's Aesop with a Substack account. "And the tree needed fire, dear reader, much like the human heart..." Yeah, I know. But stick with me. This metaphor is too damn perfect not to use.

I know a person like this.

I'd bet money you do too. Maybe you're sitting across the dinner table from one right now. Maybe you're reading this on your phone in the bathroom because it's the only room in the house where the air doesn't feel flammable.

---

I lived with a lodgepole pine for a long time. And here's the thing I didn't understand until I was out of the fire and could look at the burn scars without flinching.

The pine doesn't just need fire. It needs *you* to be the fire.

Every time I started building something stable, the fire came. Not always a big fire. Sometimes just a spark. A comment designed to land exactly where it would hurt most. A fight manufactured out of nothing that escalated until the chaos felt inevitable. A slow, patient erosion of whatever I was trying to build, delivered in a voice that sounded like it was trying to help.

"You know this isn't going to last, right?"

That sentence, said enough times by someone you love, stops sounding like pessimism and starts sounding like a prophecy. But it's neither. It's an arson kit disguised as concern.

And when the fire finally came (and it always came), the pine's cones opened right on schedule. Crisis hit, chaos bloomed, and suddenly *my* failure was the headline. The person stacking kindling around my feet for months got to play the victim of *my* fire.

If you won't ignite on your own, they'll hand you a match and tell you it's a birthday candle.

---

I used to think the fire was the problem. If I could just prevent the fire. If I could just keep things calm. Stable. Peaceful. Then the seeds would open on their own. Then the growth would happen naturally.

But that's not how lodgepole pines work. Calm doesn't open the cones. Peace doesn't melt the resin. If anything, long periods without fire make it worse. The deadfall builds up. The underbrush thickens. The forest floor becomes a tinderbox, and when the fire finally comes (and it always comes), it's not a manageable burn. It's a crown fire. It takes the whole canopy.

The longer you suppress it, the bigger the eventual explosion.

I spent years playing fire prevention for someone whose biology *required* fire. I couldn't wrap my head around it. Every time I got things stable, they'd find a spark. A fight. A crisis. Some reason to torch it all. I thought they were self-destructive. Thought they didn't want to get better. Maybe they didn't love me enough to stop lighting matches.

But a lodgepole pine isn't lighting matches because it hates the forest. It's lighting matches because that's the only way it knows how to open.

---

The lodgepole pine didn't *choose* fire. It just adapted to a reality it couldn't change. Fires were constant, unpredictable, devastating. So the pine stopped trying to survive *despite* the fire. It started building its entire damn existence *around* it. Its seeds sealed up. Its bark thinned, making it burn faster and hotter. Everything about that tree says, "Destruction is inevitable. I've built it into my operating system."

A person who grew up in chaos didn't choose chaos. But their body adapted to it. Their nervous system calibrated to it. The cortisol and adrenaline became the baseline. And eventually, calm started to feel *wrong*. Not peaceful. Wrong. Like something was missing. Like the other shoe hadn't dropped yet.

So they find the fire. Or they make one.

Not because they're broken. Because they adapted to an environment that broke them, and the adaptation was so thorough that removing the environment doesn't remove the wiring. The resin is still there. The cones are still sealed.

---

I was talking to a friend recently who has a lodgepole pine of his own. Different relationship, same species. And he asked me the question I asked myself for years:

"How do I help them stop needing the fire?"

I just looked at him. Because I've been him. I've been the guy Googling "how to help someone who keeps creating chaos" at 2am. I've been the guy rehearsing the perfect thing to say that will finally break through. I've been the guy who thought if I just loved hard enough, stayed long enough, built something stable enough, the resin would melt on its own.

Here's what I know about that question. The first thing is that it comes from love. You wouldn't be asking it if you didn't care. The second thing is that the question itself is the trap.

You can't help a lodgepole pine stop needing fire. You can't rewire 10,000 years of evolution with a conversation. You can't melt resin with patience. The tree doesn't *know* its cones are sealed. It just knows that every time things burn, something new grows. And every time things are calm, nothing happens. So from the tree's perspective, fire = growth and peace = stagnation.

And here's the part that really messes with your head: the tree isn't wrong about its own experience. Fire genuinely does open its cones. The pattern genuinely does work, in the sense that the species has survived millions of years doing exactly this.

The problem is that everyone around the tree keeps getting burned. And some of us were already on fire when we showed up.

---

Here's what I've learned, and I'm still learning it.

You can love a lodgepole pine. You can appreciate its resilience. You can marvel at how it colonizes scorched earth and turns destruction into new life. You can respect the biology without pretending the fires don't hurt.

But you cannot be the firebreak.

If you try to absorb every fire, prevent every crisis, smooth over every burn, you will exhaust yourself completely. And the tree won't even understand what you were doing. Because from its perspective, you were just standing in the way of the only mechanism it has for opening up.

The hardest thing I ever learned, and I mean harder than getting sober, harder than the felony, harder than any of it, is that some people's growth requires destruction that I am not obligated to stand in the middle of. Loving them and protecting myself from the fire are not mutually exclusive. They're actually the same act.

You can love the lodgepole pine and still step out of the burn zone.

---

There's a thing that happens in Yellowstone after a big fire. The lodgepole pines release millions of seeds. The forest floor, stripped bare and covered in nutrient-rich ash, becomes the perfect growing medium. And within 2 or 3 years, the new pines are already a foot tall. Dense. Packed. Fighting for light. Starting the whole cycle over.

And standing at the edge of the burn scar, looking at all that new growth, you'd be forgiven for thinking the fire was a good thing.

But the elk that burned? The old-growth spruce that took 200 years to reach the canopy? The meadow that used to have wildflowers? Those don't come back the same way. The lodgepole survives because it's designed for this. Everything else in the fire's path just... lost something.

The tree doesn't mourn what burned alongside it. That's not cruelty. That's biology. The tree was never wired to consider the collateral damage.

---

My friend asked me what to do. I told him what I wish someone had told me earlier.

Learn the species. Understand the biology. Accept that the resin exists and that you didn't put it there and you can't dissolve it. Decide how close to the burn zone you're willing to stand. Set that distance. Hold it. And then watch the forest with clear eyes.

Some seasons there won't be a fire and you'll think the pattern is broken. It isn't. The deadfall is just building. Some seasons the fire will be small and contained and you'll think, okay, maybe this time. And maybe it will be okay. And some seasons it will be a crown fire that takes the whole ridge and you'll need to already be standing far enough back that you're safe.

You don't get to choose when the fires come. You only get to choose where you stand when they do.

And you get to love the tree anyway. From a safe distance, with realistic expectations, knowing exactly what species you're looking at.

Because the tree won't mourn what burns alongside it.

That's not giving up. That's growing up.

---

Happy Friday. Go outside. Look at a tree. Think about what it evolved to need and whether you've been trying to change its biology instead of adjusting your distance.

 - Michael
